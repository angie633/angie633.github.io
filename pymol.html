Pymol Hemoglobin Modeling

The protein model seen below was rendered using Schrodinger's open source version of Pymol. The video captures a 360 degree overview of human hemoglobin.

    Some key features:

  • hydrogen bonds (dashed lines)
  • oxygen molecules (red ball structures)
  • tetramers (4 colored regions)
  • chemical composition of the prosthetic heme group (orange/blue stick structure)

Purpose of Modeling

This model was created as a way for me to visualize biochemical concepts, particularly to study the effects of diabetes on the body's bloodstream. One of the most common blood tests for diabetics is the A1C test, which is a measure of how much glucose adheres to hemoglobin circulating in the bloodstream. An overabundance of glucose attachment to hemoglobin leads to a higher A1C, resulting in poor bodily control of blood sugar levels and chronic underlying health conditions (stroke, heart attack, etc).

Hemoglobin Background

Hemoglobin is a versatile protein in where oxygen is able to hydrogen bond to the beta sheets, earning it the nickname of an "O2 transporter". It's quaternary structure consists of 4 tetramers (alpha and Beta) consisting of 4 polypeptide chains. These include alpha and beta tetramer chains. The prosthetic/heme group is a non=-protein group that connect the ligands of the protein. The center of the heme group consists of iron, which allows the alpha tetramers to ineract with the beta tetramers.

Glucose & Protein Interaction

The main issue with glucose interaction in the body stems from the presence of too many amino acids building up in the hemoglobin as it circulates through the blood. Primarily, excess leucine causes the liver to export too much glucose into the blood and thus elevating blood sugar levels. The body's response: increased insulin production. This increased production poses a significant health risk for Type 1 diabetic people who may need insulin injections to supplement bodily functions.
